A join's physical strategy is a decision about *how much data has to move*, not about correctness — any strategy that respects the join condition produces the same rows. Spark picks between (at least) two shapes for an inner equi-join: broadcast the smaller side whole to every executor and match locally (no shuffle of the large side at all), or shuffle both sides by key and merge sorted partitions (both sides move, both sides sort). The first is cheap when one side is small enough to fit comfortably in memory on every executor; the second is the only safe fallback when neither side is.

`spark.sql.autoBroadcastJoinThreshold` is the size (in bytes) below which Spark's planner will broadcast a side automatically, based on its own size estimate. That estimate is a *static* one at plan-build time — it doesn't know how big a `groupBy(...).agg(...)` result will actually be until the aggregation runs. This is exactly the gap Adaptive Query Execution exists to close: re-plan using real, measured sizes after a shuffle has already happened once.

Before writing any code, work out for each of the three functions: is the small side known to be small *before* execution (obviously broadcastable), forced to be large no matter what (broadcasting disabled outright), or only "turns out small" *after* aggregation runs (which is the case AQE is built for)? That distinction is the whole task.
