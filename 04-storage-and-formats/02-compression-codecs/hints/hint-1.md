Compression codecs are not interchangeable "make it smaller" buttons — they sit at different points on a speed/ratio axis. Snappy (and lz4-family codecs generally) trade ratio for raw throughput: fast to compress, fast to decompress, modest size reduction. Gzip (deflate) spends more CPU per byte to get a better ratio, but its decompression is also slower. Zstd is interesting because it exposes the tradeoff as a dial — a compression *level* — rather than forcing you to pick a different algorithm family.

Before writing any code, think about what "hot" and "archive" mean for a storage tier. Hot data gets scanned repeatedly, possibly under interactive latency budgets — what does that imply about how much you should be willing to pay in read-side CPU? Archive data gets written once and read rarely, if ever, until someone needs it — what does that imply about how much time you can spend at write time, and how much you care about read speed at all?

Nothing here is guessable from vibes. You have five variants and a benchmark harness — that's the whole point of this task. Form a hypothesis about hot-tier and archive-tier choices before you run the benchmark, then check whether your numbers agree with you.
